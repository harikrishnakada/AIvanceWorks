# Service & Solution Page Content Voice Guide

> **Purpose:** Reference guide for writing all service and solution page copy for AIvanceWorks. Based on competitive research across Accenture, Deloitte, Thoughtworks, Slalom, Nerdery, Cognizant, Infosys, Stripe, and WP Engine (April 2026).

---

## 1. Our Voice Position

### The Tone Spectrum

```
MORE FORMAL / ABSTRACT                              MORE CONVERSATIONAL / CONCRETE
|                                                                                |
Infosys  Accenture  Cognizant  Deloitte  Thoughtworks  Slalom  Nerdery  Stripe
|---------|----------|----------|---------|-------------|-------|--------|--------|
 Catalog    Manifesto  Polished    Formal     Expert       Warm    Casual   Product
```

**AIvanceWorks sits between Thoughtworks and Slalom**, with Nerdery's concreteness and Stripe's metric discipline.

### The AIvanceWorks Voice in One Sentence

> "A smart, experienced colleague who explains complex technology in terms of business outcomes, leads with the client's problem, and backs every claim with evidence."

### Voice Characteristics

| Dimension | Recommendation | Rationale |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Person** | "We" primary, "you" secondary | Creates partnership frame. "We build X so you can achieve Y" |
| **Formality** | Professional-conversational | Contractions OK. No slang. No stiff corporate passive voice. |
| **Technical depth** | Mid-level on service pages, deep on blog/whitepapers | Service pages convince; blog posts educate |
| **Jargon** | Use but contextualize | "We use event-driven architectures to make your systems respond in real time" |
| **Sentence length** | Mix short and medium. Lead with short. | Short sentences create rhythm and confidence. Long ones explain. |
| **Paragraph length** | 2-3 sentences max on service pages | Scanners decide in 8 seconds. Walls of text lose them. |
| **Confidence level** | Assertive but honest | "We've done this 50 times" beats "we're the best in the world" |
| **Humor** | Subtle, not forced | A smart observation is better than a joke |

## 2. How Top Firms Write (By Tier)

### Enterprise Voice (Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant, Infosys)

Formal, declarative, third-person-dominant. Abstract nouns over concrete verbs. Never use contractions.

**Examples:**
- Accenture: *"AI at scale depends on cloud at scale. Unlock cloud's true potential by modernizing and reframing it as the engine of AI, innovation and growth."*
- Deloitte: *"Organizations are facing new challenges and evolving technological disruptions. To drive progress, harness the power of Deloitte's AI & Engineering services."*
- Cognizant: *"Business growth requires better decision making. Trusted intelligence with data and AI services enables you to outpace dynamic markets."*

**Pattern:** Second-person imperatives embedded in third-person frameworks. "Organizations face X" (universal truth) then "unlock Y" (command).

### Mid-Market Voice (Thoughtworks, Slalom, Nerdery) -- OUR PEERS

Warmer, more direct, first-and-second-person mix. Contractions OK. Shorter sentences. Lead with the client's problem.

**Examples:**
- Thoughtworks: *"Most modernization 'upgrades' simply delay the inevitable, leading back to technical debt and high-risk overhauls. We help organizations move from reactive maintenance to a living capability."*
- Slalom: *"We don't come with predetermined processes. Rather, we roll up our sleeves and craft practical, end-to-end solutions in partnership with your teams."*
- Nerdery: *"Today, businesses need to cut costs without cutting corners. Our cloud application migration helps you leverage the cloud to reduce operational costs."*

**Pattern:** "We + you" framing. Problem named bluntly first, then solution framed as partnership.

### Product Voice (Stripe, WP Engine) -- ASPIRATION FOR CONCRETENESS

Benefit-first, metric-heavy, action-oriented. Most concrete of all.

**Examples:**
- Stripe: *"Unified, global payments to grow your revenue."* Then: *"11.9% average revenue uplift for businesses using Stripe's latest checkout optimizations."*
- WP Engine: *"Enterprise hosting, without the complexity."*

**Pattern:** Specific metrics, specific features, specific use cases. Trust through precision.

### Person Usage Reference

| Voice Style | Example |
|------------|---------|
| 2nd person imperative (Accenture) | "Unlock cloud's true potential" |
| 3rd person + 2nd person (Deloitte) | "Organizations are facing... harness the power" |
| 1st person plural (Thoughtworks) | "We help organizations move from reactive maintenance" |
| 1st person plural (Slalom) | "We combine our services to discover, design, and build" |
| Mixed we/you (Nerdery) | "Our cloud application migration helps you" |
| 2nd person benefit (Stripe) | "Accept payments... to grow your revenue" |

## 3. Content Structure Patterns

### Hero Section

**Enterprise firms:** 2-level hero (H1: category name, H2: value proposition 10-25 words, body: 1-2 sentences)

**Mid-market firms (us):** Tagline + narrative hero. Problem in sentence one, approach in sentence two, outcome in sentence three.

**Product firms:** Metric-laden heroes with immediate visual demo.

**Our rule:** Hero headline under 40 words. Subhead can elaborate.

### Page Sections (Industry Standard for Mid-Market)

1. Hero (headline + subhead + dual CTA)
2. Metrics strip (3-4 stats with short labels)
3. Feature/capability grid (card-based, H3 + 1-2 sentences + link)
4. Signature/differentiator section
5. Benefits (outcome-focused)
6. Process/methodology (visible, numbered)
7. Engagement models / pricing tier hints
8. Related pages (cross-links)
9. FAQ (5-7 questions)
10. Bottom CTA

### Feature/Service Description Format

Card-based is dominant across all firms. Each card:
- **H3:** Capability name
- **Body:** 1-2 sentences: what it is + why it matters
- **Link:** Optional "Learn more"

**Best practice:** Lead each card with a stat or hook, not just the name.

### Benefits Framing

| Approach | Who Uses It | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Outcome-focused** | Slalom, Stripe | "to grow your revenue", "to drive impact" |
| **Problem-focused** | Thoughtworks, Nerdery | "Most modernization 'upgrades' simply delay the inevitable" |
| **Capability-focused** | Accenture, Deloitte | "harness the power of AI & Engineering services" |

**Best pages layer all three:** name the problem, present the capability, state the outcome.

### CTA Patterns

| Style | Language | Count |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **Consultancy (us)** | "Let's talk," "Start a conversation," "Book a discovery call" | 2 per page (hero + bottom) |
| **Product company** | "Start now," "Try the demo," "Contact sales" | 3-5 per page |

**Our rule:** 2 CTAs per page. Soft, relational language. Never salesy.

## 4. Persuasion Techniques

### Pain vs. Aspiration

- **Enterprise firms** lead with aspirations ("Build with purpose", "AI at scale")
- **Mid-market firms (us)** lead with pain points ("Most modernization upgrades simply delay the inevitable")
- **Exception:** Slalom leads with inspiration ("AI dares us to rethink the impossible")

**Our default:** Lead with the client's pain point. Use aspiration in CTA sections.

### Credibility Layering

The best pages combine at least 3 of these 5 mechanisms:

1. **Statistics/metrics** -- specific numbers with sources
2. **Partner logos** -- AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, etc.
3. **Analyst recognition** -- Forrester, Gartner, ISO certifications
4. **Case studies** -- named clients with outcomes
5. **Leadership profiles** -- photos and credentials

**Our current state:** Strong on metrics, process visibility. Weak on named clients (greenfield). Add case studies as they become available.

### Pricing

**Industry standard for consultancies:** Hidden. Not mentioned on service pages. CTA is always "Contact us."

**Our approach:** We show engagement model tiers with "from $X" pricing. This is a differentiator -- more transparent than most consultancies. Keep this.

### Differentiation in Copy

Most consultancies do NOT explicitly differentiate. They imply it through:
- **Scale claims:** stats and partner counts
- **Methodology naming:** proprietary frameworks
- **Cultural positioning:** Slalom's "Fiercely Human", Nerdery's "Consult with a nerd"
- **Anti-positioning:** "We don't come with predetermined processes" (only explicit competitive swipe observed)

## 5. Content Length & Depth

### Word Count Guidelines

| Page Type | Target | Depth |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Service hub/listing page | 300-500 words | Shallow -- funnel to detail pages |
| Service detail page | 500-800 words of narrative + structured content | Medium |
| Solution detail page | 600-800 words of narrative + structured content | Medium-deep |
| Blog/whitepaper | 1500-3000 words | Deep |

### Technical Depth by Context

- **Service pages:** Mid-level. Name the tech, explain the outcome. Don't explain the implementation.
- **Solution pages (regulated):** Slightly deeper for compliance sections (the buyer WANTS to see that depth)
- **Blog posts:** Go deep. Code samples, architecture diagrams OK.
- **FAQs:** Detailed answers are fine -- the reader chose to expand.

## 6. Language Patterns

### Phrases to AVOID (appeared on 3+ consultancy sites)

| Phrase | Verdict | Why |
|--------|---------|-----|
| "digital transformation" | AVOID | Meaningless through overuse |
| "accelerate" / "accelerating" | CAUTION | Overused but sometimes unavoidable |
| "unlock" / "unlocking" (standalone) | AVOID | Works only if paired with specific object |
| "drive growth/innovation/results" | CAUTION | Vague when used alone |
| "empower" | AVOID | Passive and patronizing |
| "leverage" (as verb) | CAUTION | Corporate jargon |
| "harness the power of" | AVOID | Cliche |
| "end-to-end" | CAUTION | Useful but overexposed |
| "reinvent" / "reinvention" | AVOID | Unless it's your actual brand word |
| "at scale" | CAUTION | Meaningful only when specific |
| "future-ready" / "future-proof" | AVOID | Empty promise |
| "world-class" | AVOID | Unprovable |
| "solutions" (as a verb) | AVOID | It's not a verb |

### Patterns Worth Stealing

**Thoughtworks** -- "Turn legacy into leverage"
- Takes a negative word and reframes it as an asset. Surprising, specific, memorable.

**Slalom** -- "Fiercely Human Consulting"
- Two unexpected words collide with a word that implies the opposite.

**Nerdery** -- "Consult with a nerd"
- Self-deprecating, memorable, human. Signals technical depth without formality.

**Stripe** -- "11.9% average revenue uplift"
- Specific number, specific metric, no hedging. Trust through precision.

**Thoughtworks** -- "30 days saved in legacy code reverse engineering"
- Concrete outcome. Not "faster" but "30 days saved."

### What Makes Pages Feel Trustworthy

1. **Specific numbers** over vague claims. "30 days saved" > "faster delivery."
2. **Named clients** over anonymous case studies.
3. **Problems admitted** before solutions offered. Admitting industry reality builds trust.
4. **Methodology made visible.** Named frameworks signal structured thinking.

### What Makes Pages Feel Like Empty Marketing Fluff

1. **Abstract nouns without concrete referents.** "Drive efficiencies and accelerate innovation" -- what efficiencies?
2. **Capability lists without outcomes.** "We offer cloud strategy, migration, security, optimization" -- so what?
3. **Missing "so that" clause.** Every capability should pass: "We do X so that you can Y."
4. **Identical structure across every page.** Feels templated, not thought through.
5. **No concrete metrics or named clients.** Reads as aspirational fiction.

## 7. Template Sentence Structures

### Hero Headline Patterns

```
[Outcome in 5-12 words]
  "Turn a fuzzy idea into a plan you can ship."
  "From kickoff to paying customers in 12 weeks."
  "Claims in hours, not weeks."

[Problem-contrast pattern]
  "Enterprise hosting, without the complexity."
  "Patient portals that patients actually use — and auditors actually approve."

[Tagline + expansion]
  "Turn legacy into leverage."
  "Headless commerce, built for performance — and owned by you."
```

### Problem-Solution-Outcome (Feature/Benefit Descriptions)

```
[Problem]. [How we solve it]. [What you get].

"Legacy codebases slow every release and frustrate your best engineers.
We refactor and migrate to cloud-native architectures using a proven 6-step process.
You get faster deployments, lower infrastructure costs, and a team that ships with confidence."
```

### Stat-Backed Claim

```
[Stat with source]. [What it means for the reader]. [What we do about it].

"Only 42% of companies report achieving expected returns from cloud.
It takes a custom strategy to meet your unique goals.
Learn how to make the most of cloud's potential."
```

### Capability Card

```
[Capability Name]
[Outcome-focused headline, 8-12 words]
[1-2 sentences: what it is + why it matters]

Example:
"Application Transformation
Become a world-class software organization
40% of companies cite outdated applications as a key barrier to cloud value.
Adaptability is essential. Here's how to transform your apps and unlock new potential."
```

### CTA Block

```
[Aspirational statement or question]. [Soft CTA].

"Let's figure out what you're actually building." / "Book free intake call"
"Ready to put working software in real users' hands?" / "Book intake call"
"Let's solve together." / "Get in touch"
```

### Testimonial

```
"[Specific outcome or praise, 1-2 sentences]"
-- [Full Name], [Title], [Company]
```

## 8. Copy Quality Tests

Every sentence of service/solution page copy should pass these three tests:

1. **The "so what" test:** Does it state an outcome the reader cares about?
2. **The "prove it" test:** Is there a metric, case study, or named example nearby?
3. **The "only us" test:** Could a competitor say this exact sentence? If yes, rewrite it.

## 9. Do's and Don'ts Checklist

### DO

1. Lead with the client's problem. "Your legacy systems are slowing down every sprint" > "We offer legacy modernization services."
2. State outcomes in the first 40-60 words. AEO and featured snippets demand clear answers upfront.
3. Use specific metrics. "Reduced deployment time from 3 weeks to 2 days" is 10x more persuasive than "faster deployments."
4. Name clients whenever possible. Even "a mid-market healthcare SaaS company" is better than nothing.
5. Show your methodology. A numbered process, a framework diagram, or a named approach signals structured thinking.
6. Use the "problem / approach / outcome" pattern for feature descriptions and case study summaries.
7. Place 2 CTAs per page -- one in/near the hero, one at the bottom. Soft language: "Let's talk," "Start a conversation."
8. Include a metrics strip near the top (3-4 stats with short labels).
9. Layer credibility types. Don't rely on just logos or just case studies. Combine: stats + named client + methodology.
10. Write scannable sub-service descriptions. Card format: H3, 1-2 sentences, link.

### DON'T

1. Don't open with your company name. "AIvanceWorks provides..." is narcissistic. Open with the client's world.
2. Don't use unprovable superlatives. "World-class," "best-in-class," "industry-leading" -- unless you have the analyst report to prove it.
3. Don't list capabilities without outcomes. Every capability needs a "so that" clause.
4. Don't hide behind passive voice. "Digital transformation is delivered" -- by whom? Say "We deliver" or "You get."
5. Don't use "solutions" as a verb ("we solution your challenges").
6. Don't stuff the hero with more than 40 words. The body paragraph can elaborate.
7. Don't mention pricing on service pages (industry standard). Exception: our engagement models with "from $X" are a deliberate transparency differentiator -- keep these.
8. Don't use the same hero structure on every page. Vary between problem-lead, aspiration-lead, and metric-lead heroes.
9. Don't forget mobile. Cards should be scannable at 375px.
10. Don't write more than 800 words of narrative on a single service page.

## 10. Anti-Patterns Table

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Too enterprise-y** | "Organizations must harness the transformative power of cloud-native architectures to drive digital reinvention at scale" | Sounds like it was written by committee. No human voice. |
| **Too salesy** | "Our world-class team of experts delivers unparalleled results that exceed expectations" | Unprovable superlatives. Zero information content. |
| **Too generic** | "We help businesses with cloud, AI, and digital transformation" | Could describe 10,000 companies. No differentiation. |
| **Too casual** | "Cloud migration? Yeah, we got you. Hit us up and let's make it happen!" | Undermines credibility for B2B buyers making 6-7 figure decisions. |
| **Too academic** | "Leveraging a microservices-based event-driven architecture pattern with domain-driven design principles..." | Service page, not a technical blog. Save depth for the right context. |
| **Name-dropping without substance** | "We partner with AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Databricks, Snowflake, Terraform, Kubernetes..." | Logo soup without explaining what you actually do differently. |
